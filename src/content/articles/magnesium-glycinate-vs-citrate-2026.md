## Magnesium Glycinate: The Form for Sleep and Long-Term Use

**Best for: people supplementing for sleep quality, muscle cramps, anxiety, or sustained magnesium repletion**

Glycinate is magnesium chelated to glycine, an amino acid with its own documented calming properties. You get two effects from one capsule: magnesium's role in muscle relaxation and GABA receptor activity, plus glycine's direct effect on sleep onset and core body temperature regulation. That combination is why glycinate has become the dominant form recommended by sleep researchers over citrate.

The chelation also means the magnesium is physically shielded from competing minerals in your gut, which improves uptake compared to non-chelated forms like oxide or plain citrate salts.

## Magnesium Citrate: The Form for Digestive Support and Fast Onset

**Best for: people managing chronic constipation, occasional supplementers, or anyone who prefers a fast-acting powder drink**

Citrate is magnesium bound to citric acid. It's less expensive to produce than chelated glycinate, dissolves readily in water, and has a mild osmotic laxative effect at higher doses. That laxative effect is a legitimate therapeutic benefit if you're supplementing for bowel regularity — and a real problem if you take it before bed without expecting consequences.

At doses below 200mg elemental, most people experience nothing beyond the standard magnesium effects. Push past 300–350mg and the urgency becomes difficult to ignore. Plan accordingly.

## FAQ

**Is magnesium glycinate or citrate better for sleep?**
Glycinate, clearly. The glycine component directly supports sleep onset and core body temperature regulation — citrate has no equivalent mechanism. Take 200–400mg elemental magnesium glycinate 30–60 minutes before bed for the clearest effect.

**Will magnesium citrate cause diarrhea?**
It can, especially above 300mg elemental. The osmotic laxative effect is dose-dependent. Most people tolerate 150–200mg without issue. If you've had citrate cause urgency before, switch to glycinate — it has essentially no laxative effect at standard supplemental doses.

**How long until I notice magnesium working?**
Sleep and muscle effects often appear within the first week. Restoring genuinely depleted magnesium levels takes 4–6 weeks of consistent daily supplementation. A serum magnesium test from your doctor (typically $25–$40 as an add-on lab) can confirm your baseline before you start.

**Can I take magnesium glycinate and citrate together?**
Yes, but there's no meaningful benefit to stacking two well-absorbed forms. You'd be adding complexity without gaining anything you couldn't get by simply taking a higher dose of one form. Pick one, dial in your dose, and stay consistent.

**Is prescription magnesium citrate the same as the supplement?**
No. Pharmacy bowel-prep preparations like Citroma deliver roughly 1,745mg elemental magnesium in a single bottle — approximately 10 times a typical supplement serving. Those are procedural laxatives, not daily supplements. The doses discussed in this article are far lower and serve an entirely different purpose.
