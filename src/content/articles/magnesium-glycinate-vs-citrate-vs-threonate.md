## What the Science Actually Says

Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including ATP synthesis, protein synthesis, DNA repair, and neuromuscular signaling. [Study: de Baaij et al., 2015, Physiological Reviews] The U.S. RDA sits at 400–420mg/day for adult men and 310–320mg/day for women, but population surveys consistently show that approximately 48% of Americans fall below these targets from diet alone. [Study: Rosanoff et al., 2012, Nutrition Reviews]

The form you take determines how much magnesium actually reaches your tissues. The differences in bioavailability between forms are large enough to matter clinically — and large enough that the wrong choice means you're essentially not supplementing at all.

### Magnesium Glycinate

Glycinate is magnesium chelated to glycine — an amino acid with its own calming properties through NMDA receptor modulation. The chelation protects the magnesium molecule through the stomach's acidic environment, and glycine acts as an intestinal carrier that improves uptake through specialized transport proteins.

**Bioavailability is high — roughly 80% in comparative studies, against oxide's 4%.** [Study: Walker et al., 2003, J Am Coll Nutr] Glycinate is also the least likely form to cause loose stools because very little escapes absorption in the small intestine to reach the colon and exert osmotic effects.

The sleep benefit is well-supported. Magnesium regulates melatonin synthesis and upregulates GABA — the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter governing sleep onset. A randomized controlled trial in elderly adults with poor sleep found magnesium supplementation significantly reduced insomnia severity index scores and improved subjective sleep quality. [Study: Abbasi et al., 2012, J Res Med Sci] The glycine component adds a separate sleep-promoting effect — glycine at 3g has independently shown reduced sleep latency in RCT conditions. [Study: Bannai et al., 2012, Sleep Biol Rhythms]

**Effective studied dose: 200–400mg elemental magnesium per day.** Always look for the elemental content on the label, not just the compound weight. A 1,000mg tablet of magnesium glycinate contains roughly 140mg elemental magnesium — far less than the number on the front of the bottle implies.

### Magnesium Citrate

Citrate is magnesium bound to citric acid. Bioavailability is comparable to glycinate — around 70–80% — but the absorption mechanism differs. Citrate dissociates in the gut, and free magnesium ions are absorbed via paracellular and active transport pathways across the intestinal wall.

The clinically relevant difference: **citrate retains osmotic activity in the colon.** This is why high-dose magnesium citrate is used as a bowel prep, and why therapeutic doses of ~300mg/day reliably improve stool frequency in constipated individuals. [Study: Mori et al., 2017, Eur J Nutr] This is a therapeutic feature if you need it — a side effect if you don't.

Magnesium generally shows solid cardiovascular data: a meta-analysis of 34 RCTs found supplementation reduced diastolic blood pressure by 2–3 mmHg. [Study: Kass et al., 2012, Eur J Clin Nutr] Citrate was among the most commonly used forms in those included studies.

**Effective dose: 200–400mg elemental magnesium per day.** Start low — 100–150mg — and titrate up over one to two weeks to avoid GI urgency.

### Magnesium L-Threonate

Threonate is a metabolite of vitamin C (ascorbic acid). When magnesium is chelated to it, the resulting compound — commercially sold as Magtein, originally developed from MIT laboratory research — has documented blood-brain-barrier crossing activity that other magnesium forms lack.

**The mechanism:** threonate leverages glucose transporter proteins (GLUT1) expressed on the blood-brain barrier to carry magnesium into the CNS. Other forms don't engage this pathway effectively, which is why cerebrospinal fluid magnesium concentrations remain largely unchanged even with high-dose supplementation of other forms.

The foundational animal study demonstrated that magnesium L-threonate elevated brain magnesium levels by 7–15% compared to other forms and improved synaptic plasticity markers. [Study: Slutsky et al., 2010, Neuron] Human data is more limited but meaningful. A 2016 RCT in cognitively impaired older adults showed significant improvements in executive function and working memory scores after 12 weeks at 2,000mg Magtein/day (delivering ~144mg elemental Mg). [Study: Liu et al., 2016, J Alzheimers Dis]

**The limitation is real:** the studied dose is 2,000mg of the compound daily, delivering only ~144mg elemental magnesium. You are not meaningfully filling your daily magnesium RDA with threonate alone. You are paying for CNS targeting and licensed IP — and those things cost money.

If cognitive health and brain aging are your primary concerns, threonate is the justified choice. For sleep and general magnesium repletion, glycinate wins on every dimension except this one.

## Dosing and Timing Guide

**When to take it:** All three forms are best taken in the evening, 30–60 minutes before bed. Magnesium supports GABA upregulation and melatonin synthesis — both part of the physiological transition into sleep. Evening timing aligns supplementation with when these pathways are most active.

**Do not take magnesium with calcium at the same time in large doses.** At high doses, they compete for intestinal transporters. Separate by at least two hours if you're supplementing both at meaningful amounts (over 500mg calcium combined).

**There is no loading protocol for magnesium** the way creatine has one. Tissue stores replete over four to eight weeks of consistent supplementation. Do not judge a magnesium supplement on the first week.

**Dosing by form:**
- **Glycinate/Bisglycinate:** Start at 150–200mg elemental Mg per night. You can increase to 300–400mg split as 200mg morning and 200mg evening if needed. Most people see maximal sleep benefit in the 200–300mg/night range.
- **Citrate:** Start at one softgel (~100mg elemental) and titrate up over two weeks to avoid GI urgency. Target 200mg/night once tolerating it well.
- **L-Threonate (Magtein):** Follow the studied protocol — 3 capsules delivering 2,000mg Magtein per day. Some people split as 1 capsule morning + 2 capsules at night. The published cognitive RCT used once-nightly dosing.

**Stacking notes:**

Magnesium stacks well with several common supplements. **Vitamin D3** — both support bone mineralization, and D3 increases magnesium utilization, making co-supplementation rational for most people. **Zinc** — frequently deficient alongside magnesium, but high-dose zinc (50mg+) can impair magnesium absorption; keep supplemental zinc under 30mg if stacking. **L-theanine** — complements glycinate's calming properties for sleep; 200mg glycinate with 100–200mg theanine is a well-tolerated evening combination. **Apigenin** — gaining popularity for sleep support; no known adverse interaction with magnesium.

## Who Should and Shouldn't Take Magnesium

**Who benefits most:**

- **Poor sleepers** — the GABA and melatonin pathway support is the most replicated clinical finding across magnesium forms; it's a reasonable first intervention before more aggressive sleep aids
- **Adults over 50** — magnesium absorption efficiency decreases with age and dietary intake tends to decline; this population is disproportionately deficient
- **High-stress individuals** — cortisol chronically depletes intracellular magnesium stores; stress and deficiency create a feedback loop
- **Athletes** — heavy training increases magnesium losses through sweat and urine by 10–20% [Study: Zhang et al., 2017, Nutrients]
- **People on proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)** — long-term PPI use impairs magnesium absorption; supplementation is often medically warranted and sometimes necessary
- **Individuals with type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance** — meta-analyses show modest but consistent improvements in insulin sensitivity with magnesium supplementation [Study: Veronese et al., 2016, Eur J Clin Nutr]

**Who should exercise caution:**

- **Kidney disease (CKD stage 3+):** Impaired kidneys cannot excrete excess magnesium, risking hypermagnesemia — a potentially fatal condition. Do not supplement without medical clearance.
- **Antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines):** Magnesium chelates these drugs and reduces antibiotic absorption. Separate doses by at least two hours.
- **Diuretics:** Thiazide and loop diuretics cause magnesium wasting; supplementation may be needed but dosing requires medical guidance since requirements vary significantly.
- **Digoxin:** Magnesium affects cardiac conduction; drug interaction risk is real and clinically meaningful.
- **Myasthenia gravis:** Magnesium can impair neuromuscular transmission in this condition.

**Absolute contraindications:**
- Active bowel obstruction — citrate especially
- Established hypermagnesemia, regardless of cause

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best form of magnesium for sleep?

**Magnesium glycinate is the most evidence-supported form for sleep.** Magnesium regulates melatonin synthesis and upregulates GABA-mediated inhibitory signaling — both critical for sleep onset and maintenance. [Study: Abbasi et al., 2012, J Res Med Sci] The glycine component adds an independent sleep-promoting effect through core body temperature reduction and its own CNS calming properties. Citrate works mechanically but its GI activity can disrupt sleep in sensitive individuals. Threonate has shown sleep-related effects in animal studies, but the human sleep-specific data is less robust than for general magnesium supplementation.

### What is the difference between magnesium glycinate and bisglycinate?

Standard glycinate chelates one glycine molecule per magnesium ion; bisglycinate chelates two. The double chelation theoretically provides more complete protection through the stomach's acidic environment. In practice, the real-world bioavailability difference in humans is modest — both forms significantly outperform magnesium oxide, but the gap between glycinate and bisglycinate is small in the comparative studies that exist. [Study: Walker et al., 2003, J Am Coll Nutr] The choice mostly comes down to brand trust, tablet versus capsule preference, and whether the glycine content is meaningful to you.

### Does magnesium L-threonate actually cross the blood-brain barrier?

Yes — this is the most scientifically credible differentiating claim in the magnesium supplement category. The 2010 MIT study demonstrated significantly elevated cerebrospinal fluid magnesium concentrations in animals supplemented with L-threonate that were not observed with other magnesium forms, including glycinate and citrate. [Study: Slutsky et al., 2010, Neuron] The proposed mechanism involves GLUT1 glucose transporter proteins expressed on the blood-brain barrier. Human neuroimaging data is limited, but the 2016 RCT in cognitively impaired older adults showed functional cognitive improvements at 2,000mg/day Magtein, which supports the mechanistic claim indirectly. Whether this translates to meaningful benefit in healthy younger adults remains less established.

### Can you take magnesium glycinate and L-threonate together?

Yes — the two forms target different mechanisms and don't compete for absorption or downstream action. A common protocol is glycinate (200mg elemental Mg) at night for sleep and general repletion, plus L-threonate (2,000mg Magtein) for cognitive support. Combined elemental magnesium would be roughly 340–350mg — within safe range for most adults with healthy kidneys. Both glycinate and threonate are low-laxative forms, so additive GI effects are unlikely at these doses.

### How long does it take for magnesium to work for sleep?

Most people notice measurable sleep improvements within **one to two weeks** of consistent evening supplementation, though full tissue repletion takes four to eight weeks. If you were genuinely deficient entering supplementation, the effect tends to be faster and more pronounced. A 2012 RCT in elderly adults with insomnia found statistically significant improvements in the Insomnia Severity Index by week four at 500mg/day. [Study: Abbasi et al., 2012, J Res Med Sci] Give any magnesium supplement a full four weeks before drawing conclusions — first-week effects often reflect GI adjustment more than true CNS response.

### Is magnesium safe to take every day long-term?

For most adults, 200–400mg supplemental elemental magnesium daily is safe indefinitely. The NIH sets the Tolerable Upper Intake Level for supplemental magnesium at **350mg/day** — above this, GI effects become more likely, though true toxicity requires much higher doses in people with healthy kidneys. [Source: NIH Office of Dietary Supplements] Healthy kidneys excrete excess magnesium efficiently. The exception is kidney disease: impaired excretion allows dangerous magnesium accumulation even at supplemental doses. If you have any kidney function concerns, get a baseline eGFR checked before starting long-term supplementation.

### Why does magnesium oxide appear in so many supplements if it barely absorbs?

Cost. Magnesium oxide is five to ten times cheaper per kilogram than glycinate or threonate, and it has the highest elemental magnesium percentage by weight at 60% — so manufacturers can print large milligram numbers on the label using very little raw material. A 500mg magnesium oxide capsule delivers roughly 20mg of usable magnesium at roughly 4% bioavailability. [Study: Firoz & Graber, 2001, Magnes Res] For context, a small handful of pumpkin seeds delivers more absorbable magnesium. Check the form listed on your current supplement before assuming you're covered — oxide is largely inert at standard supplement doses.
