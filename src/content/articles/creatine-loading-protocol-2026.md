## What the Science Actually Says

### The Physiology of Creatine Saturation

Creatine works by increasing phosphocreatine (PCr) stores in skeletal muscle, which accelerates ATP resynthesis during maximal efforts lasting roughly 1–10 seconds. Resting muscle creatine stores run around 120–130 mmol/kg dry mass in most meat-eating adults, but the ceiling for supplementation is approximately 150–160 mmol/kg. [Study: Harris et al., 1992]

The loading debate centers entirely on how quickly you reach that ceiling, not whether you reach it.

**The standard loading protocol** — 20g/day split into 4×5g doses for 5–7 days, then 3–5g/day maintenance — saturates muscle creatine stores in approximately seven days. [Study: Hultman et al., 1996] Split dosing matters: a single 20g bolus overwhelms renal reabsorption capacity and a significant fraction is excreted unused.

**The no-loading approach** — 3–5g/day from day one — reaches the identical saturation endpoint, just more slowly: approximately 21–28 days. [Study: Burke et al., 2003]

### Does Loading Actually Matter for Performance?

Yes, but only in the short term. A meta-analysis in the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research found that loading accelerated strength and power improvements in the first two weeks compared to maintenance-only dosing, but showed no statistically significant difference between groups by week four. [Study: Lanhers et al., 2017]

**The practical rule**: load if you're two weeks out from a competition or testing event. Skip loading if you're starting a 12-week training block — the endpoint is identical.

### GI Side Effects: The Hidden Cost of Loading

Approximately 30% of users report meaningful GI distress — bloating, cramping, loose stools — on the 20g/day loading protocol. [Study: Greenhaff et al., 1993] This is more prevalent when doses are taken in a fasted state or without adequate hydration.

Spreading 20g across 4–5 meals substantially reduces symptoms but doesn't eliminate them for everyone. If you have IBS or a sensitive gut, the maintenance-only approach is almost always the better call.

### Creatine Monohydrate vs. Alternatives

Despite heavy marketing for creatine HCl, Kre-Alkalyn, buffered creatine, and micronized variants, **creatine monohydrate remains the only form with consistent, replicated evidence** across strength, power, and body composition outcomes. A 2021 review in Nutrients found no significant advantage for any alternative form over monohydrate when matched for actual creatine content. [Study: Jagim et al., 2021]

Creatine HCl dissolves better in water — a genuine practical advantage — but this solubility difference doesn't translate to meaningfully better muscle saturation at matched doses. You're paying more for a mixing property, not a performance edge.

## Dosing and Timing Guide

### Loading Protocol (7-Day Saturation)

If you need peak muscle creatine stores within one week — pre-competition, peaking for a strength test, or starting a new training block and wanting the performance benefit now:

**Days 1–7 (Loading Phase):**
- **20g/day**, split into **4 doses of 5g** spaced 3–4 hours apart
- Take each dose with food and a carbohydrate source — insulin release enhances creatine transport into muscle cells [Study: Green et al., 1996]
- Aim for an extra 500ml of water daily during loading week to minimize GI side effects
- Expect 0.5–2kg of weight gain by day 7, primarily intramuscular water — this is the mechanism working, not fat accumulation

**Day 8 onward (Maintenance Phase):**
- **3–5g/day** — once daily is sufficient; split dosing is no longer necessary
- Timing is largely irrelevant; take it at whatever time you'll consistently remember

### No-Loading Protocol (Recommended for Most People)

Take **5g/day** from day one. You'll reach full muscle saturation by days 21–28. This approach eliminates loading-phase GI discomfort, wastes no product, and the performance difference from loading resolves completely by week four. Unless you have a specific short-term deadline, this is the default.

### Should You Cycle Creatine?

No. There's no peer-reviewed evidence supporting cyclical on/off protocols. Stopping supplementation allows muscle stores to deplete over 4–6 weeks — you lose the saturation advantage with no physiological benefit from the break. Take it daily, indefinitely, unless your physician recommends otherwise.

### Stacking Notes

Creatine combines well with caffeine (the proposed antagonism between the two has been largely refuted in meta-analyses [Study: Trexler & Smith-Ryan, 2015]), protein, and beta-alanine. ## Who Should and Shouldn't Take Creatine

### Populations With the Strongest Evidence

- **Strength and power athletes:** Evidence is clearest for short-duration, high-intensity efforts — maximal lifts, sprints, high-velocity resistance training. Effect sizes for strength and power are consistently meaningful across dozens of trials. [Study: Rawson & Venezia, 2011]
- **Vegetarians and vegans:** Baseline muscle creatine stores average 20–30% lower in non-meat-eaters. Relative gains from supplementation are often larger in this group than in omnivores.
- **Aging adults (50+):** Emerging research suggests creatine may attenuate age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia) and support cognitive function — benefits that extend beyond the gym. [Study: Candow et al., 2021]
- **Athletes returning from injury or deload:** Loading during return-to-training can help accelerate strength reacquisition by restoring PCr stores quickly.

### Who Should Exercise Caution

- **People with diagnosed kidney disease:** Creatine metabolism produces creatinine, which artificially elevates kidney function markers on standard blood panels. In healthy adults this is benign, but in CKD patients or those with a single functioning kidney, discuss with your nephrologist before supplementing.
- **Those taking diuretics or NSAIDs regularly:** Mild fluid retention from creatine can interact with diuretic dosing thresholds in ways your prescribing physician should know about.
- **Individuals with IBS or significant GI sensitivity:** Use the maintenance-only protocol — never load — and always take doses with food.
- **Pregnant women:** Insufficient safety data exists for routine supplementation during pregnancy. The research is ongoing and not yet cleared for standard recommendation.

### Who Probably Doesn't Need It

**Endurance-only athletes** training at steady-state efforts exceeding three minutes see minimal benefit. The phosphocreatine energy system contributes negligibly to marathon running, long-course cycling, or sustained rowing. Some evidence supports creatine for the high-intensity interval portions within endurance training, but the effect size is much smaller than in power sports and may not justify the cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does creatine loading damage your kidneys?

In healthy adults with normal kidney function, no. Multiple systematic reviews — including a comprehensive analysis in Molecular Nutrition and Food Research — found no adverse renal effects in participants without pre-existing kidney disease at doses up to 20g/day. [Study: Gualano et al., 2012] The confusion arises because creatine metabolism elevates serum creatinine, a routine kidney function marker. Your doctor may flag the number on a blood panel, but it reflects metabolic load, not kidney damage. If you have pre-existing kidney disease, discuss with your nephrologist before supplementing.

### Is 3g per day enough, or do I need 5g?

Research supports 3g/day as sufficient for meaningful creatine saturation over time, but **5g/day** is the most frequently studied maintenance dose and provides more buffer across different body compositions. Heavier athletes (90kg+) may saturate slightly faster at 5g. Unless you're aggressively cost-optimizing, 5g is the practical standard that aligns with the bulk of the literature.

### Does timing matter — pre or post workout?

Marginally. A 2013 study in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition found a small advantage for post-workout creatine versus pre-workout, but the study was small and unblinded. [Study: Antonio & Ciccone, 2013] The honest answer is: take it when you'll actually remember to take it every day. Consistency beats optimizing a 10-minute timing window.

### Can I take creatine with coffee or caffeine?

Yes. Earlier research suggested caffeine antagonized creatine's ergogenic effects via opposing mechanisms on muscle relaxation time. A subsequent meta-analysis in the European Journal of Sport Science found no meaningful antagonism between caffeine and creatine when both are taken consistently with adequate hydration. [Study: Trexler & Smith-Ryan, 2015] Stack them freely — the interaction concern appears to have been overstated in early literature.

### What's the actual difference between creatine monohydrate and creatine HCl?

Creatine HCl dissolves more readily in water and causes less GI discomfort at equivalent doses — both genuine practical advantages. The proposed mechanism is that superior absorption means you need less of it (1–2g versus 5g monohydrate). This claim hasn't been convincingly demonstrated in controlled trials. A 2021 review in Nutrients found **no significant advantage for creatine HCl over monohydrate** for strength, power, or body composition outcomes when matched for creatine content. [Study: Jagim et al., 2021] Monohydrate remains the evidence-backed choice.

### Does creatine cause water retention and bloating?

Creatine causes **intramuscular water retention** — water drawn into muscle cells — which actually contributes to the volumization and strength effect. This is mechanistically distinct from subcutaneous water retention. Most people gain 0.5–2kg in the first week of loading, primarily from intramuscular water. Loading-associated bloating is a separate GI issue caused by high-dose ingestion; it typically resolves when dropping to maintenance dosing and spacing doses with meals.

### Should I take creatine while cutting?

Yes — arguably more so than during a bulk. Creatine's ability to maintain phosphocreatine availability supports training quality when caloric restriction reduces overall output. Research in the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research found creatine supplementation helped preserve lean mass during hypocaloric conditions. [Study: Rawson & Venezia, 2003] This is also the context where HMB combinations have the strongest theoretical rationale — though the underdosing issue with Transparent Labs' product still applies if you go that route.
