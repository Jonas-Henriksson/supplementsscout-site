## What the Science Actually Says

Collagen makes up roughly 70–80% of tendon dry weight and approximately 60% of ligament dry weight. Unlike muscle protein synthesis — which responds robustly to resistance training within 24–48 hours — connective tissue collagen turnover is slower and significantly more dependent on nutritional timing and specific co-factors.

The foundational study on pre-workout collagen timing is **Shaw et al. (2017)** [Study: Shaw et al., 2017, American Journal of Clinical Nutrition]. Researchers gave 8 healthy males either 5g, 15g, or placebo of vitamin C-enriched gelatin, then had them perform 6 minutes of rope-skipping one hour later. Using amino acid tracer analysis to measure collagen synthesis in engineered ligament tissue, they found a **dose-dependent increase at the 15g dose**, with significantly elevated synthesis markers versus placebo. This is the origin of the "15g, 1 hour before exercise" recommendation that sports dietitians now cite regularly.

A more recent 2023 resistance-training study [Study: Clifford et al., 2023, ScienceDirect] raised the dose question further. Participants receiving **30g of hydrolyzed collagen peptides showed greater PINP (procollagen type I N-terminal propeptide) synthesis markers** than both the 15g and placebo groups following a lower-body resistance session. The 15g group outperformed placebo, but the 30g group produced the largest response. If you're managing an active injury, the higher dose is worth considering.

**The mechanism:** hydrolyzed collagen peptides are broken down into small peptides — particularly hydroxyproline-containing dipeptides — that appear in circulating blood within 30–60 minutes of ingestion and show preferential accumulation in connective tissue. Exercise creates the mechanical loading signal that upregulates collagen synthesis genes in tendons and ligaments. The timing protocol aligns peak amino acid availability with this mechanical stimulus.

### The vitamin C requirement is non-negotiable

Collagen synthesis requires two vitamin C-dependent enzymes — **prolyl hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase** — to hydroxylate proline and lysine residues into a stable triple helix structure. Without adequate vitamin C, these hydroxylation reactions are impaired and newly synthesized collagen fibers are structurally weak. Shaw's 2017 protocol explicitly used vitamin C alongside gelatin; most subsequent positive studies have replicated this pairing. The majority of collagen powders on the market do not include vitamin C, meaning you must add it yourself — or choose a product like Gnarly Collagen Pro that includes it.

### Where the evidence is weaker

The picture is not uniformly positive. Research from **Luc Van Loon's group** found no significant benefit of twice-daily 15g collagen supplementation versus placebo for tendon cross-sectional area and mechanical properties over 12 weeks of intense resistance training in well-trained young males. The evidence is strongest for injury rehabilitation and connective tissue repair, not for structural adaptation in already-healthy tendons.

The populations where evidence is strongest:
- **Injury rehabilitation** (tendon, ligament, cartilage repair)
- Athletes with repetitive connective tissue stress (runners, jumpers, racket sport athletes)
- People with existing joint complaints or returning from lower-limb injuries
- Middle-aged and older active adults where natural collagen production has declined

## Dosing and Timing Guide

**The evidence-based protocol:**
- **Dose:** 15–30g hydrolyzed collagen peptides
- **Timing:** 45–60 minutes before exercise
- **Vitamin C:** 80–200mg in the same pre-exercise window
- **Exercise stimulus:** Required — mechanical loading must follow within the hour

**Vitamin C pairing options:**
- 8 oz of orange juice alongside collagen (~70–80mg vitamin C)
- 100mg vitamin C tablet dissolved in water with the collagen
- Gnarly Collagen Pro (100% DV vitamin C built in, no separate purchase needed)
- Vital Proteins Advanced (includes vitamin C + hyaluronic acid)

**On dose:** 15g is the minimum threshold established in Shaw et al. (2017). The 2023 Clifford et al. data suggests **30g may produce a meaningfully stronger PINP collagen synthesis response** than 15g, particularly in a resistance training context. If you're managing an active tendon or ligament injury, upgrading to 30g is a defensible protocol change based on current evidence — the cost doubles, but for injury management the potential upside is significant.

**Loading protocol:** No established collagen-specific loading phase exists. Consistent daily use appears more important than front-loading. Most positive studies ran daily supplementation for 4–12 weeks before measuring outcomes.

**Exercise type:** Low-impact mechanical loading — rope-skipping, running, resistance training, and sport-specific movements — all appear to provide the necessary stimulus. The key requirement is that exercise follows collagen ingestion within the one-hour window. Passive stretching alone appears insufficient.

**Stacking:** Pre-workout collagen is compatible with caffeine, creatine, and most pre-workout formulas. If you're looking for a natural pre-workout to pair this protocol with, see [Best Natural Pre-Workout 2026: No Artificial Sweeteners, Colors, or Flavors](/best-natural-pre-workout-2026).

**What to avoid stacking:** High-dose antioxidants immediately post-workout (vitamin E >400 IU, vitamin C >1g) may blunt exercise adaptation signaling. The pre-workout vitamin C in the collagen protocol is a small, targeted dose — not antioxidant megadosing.

## Who Should and Shouldn't Take This

### Who benefits most

- **Injury rehabilitation:** The strongest evidence base — tendon, ligament, and cartilage injuries, particularly patellar tendinopathy, Achilles tendon issues, ligament repair post-surgery, and plantar fasciitis, are where the pre-workout timing protocol has the clearest clinical rationale.
- **Repetitive-load athletes:** Runners, cyclists, jumpers, and racket sport players who accumulate connective tissue stress through high-frequency training.
- **Middle-aged and older active adults:** Natural collagen production declines with age, and connective tissue recovery slows. Supplementation may partially offset this.
- **People returning from soft-tissue injuries:** As one user on Pillar Performance's product page noted: *"On the mend from a distal hamstring tendon tear and I swear by the impact of Collagen Repair"* — though this comes from a brand-hosted review, not an independent forum, and should be taken with appropriate caution.

### Who sees limited benefit

- **Healthy, well-trained athletes for pure injury prevention:** The Van Loon group data suggests structural tendon adaptations are difficult to demonstrate in this population. The protocol is not harmful here — just less supported by evidence for structural change.
- **Anyone seeking muscle hypertrophy support:** Collagen is not a complete protein. It **lacks tryptophan** and contains minimal branched-chain amino acids. Do not count collagen toward your daily protein target for muscle building — use whey, casein, or a complete plant protein for that purpose.
- **People taking it without exercising in the window:** The mechanical loading stimulus is required. If you're not exercising within 60 minutes of taking collagen, you may not be activating the key mechanism that makes pre-workout timing valuable.

### Contraindications and cautions

- **Fish allergy:** Marine collagen is contraindicated. Bovine collagen from certified facilities is generally safe.
- **Phenylketonuria (PKU):** Some collagen products contain phenylalanine — check labels carefully.
- **Hypercalcemia:** Bone broth-based collagen products can contain calcium. Check if you're managing elevated calcium levels.
- **Pregnancy:** No established safety concerns at physiological doses, but consult a physician before adding any new supplement regimen.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does pre-workout collagen timing actually work?

The evidence is more solid than most supplement timing claims, but with important caveats. Shaw et al. (2017) [Study: Shaw et al., 2017, AJCN] demonstrated increased collagen synthesis markers in ligament tissue when participants consumed 15g gelatin + vitamin C one hour before rope-skipping versus placebo. A 2023 study [Study: Clifford et al., 2023, ScienceDirect] found dose-dependent effects up to 30g. However, research from Van Loon's lab found no structural tendon changes in healthy, well-trained athletes over 12 weeks. The protocol is best supported for **injury rehabilitation and connective tissue repair** — not general injury prevention in already-healthy athletes.

### Why does almost no collagen supplement include vitamin C?

Partly cost, partly market convention. Most collagen brands have historically targeted the morning beauty routine — where no specific timing or co-factor pairing is required — and adding vitamin C was simply not part of the standard formulation. Sports-focused brands like Gnarly have been faster to adopt the pre-workout positioning, which makes built-in vitamin C a natural product differentiator. For existing products without it, the fix is simple: a 100mg vitamin C tablet or 8 oz of orange juice alongside your collagen is sufficient.

### Should I take collagen before or after a workout?

The evidence points to **before** — specifically 45–60 minutes pre-exercise. The mechanism requires circulating hydroxyproline-containing peptides to be elevated during the mechanical loading window. Shaw's study used a strict one-hour pre-exercise window; subsequent research has used similar or slightly shorter windows (30–45 minutes) with comparable results. Post-workout collagen is better than nothing but bypasses the timing advantage that makes this protocol worth following.

### Is 15g enough, or should I take 30g?

Both have evidence support. Shaw et al. (2017) showed significant benefit at 15g versus placebo and 5g. The 2023 Clifford et al. data found 30g outperformed 15g on PINP synthesis markers after resistance training. **15g is a reasonable starting point.** If you're managing an active tendon or ligament injury, upgrading to 30g is a defensible protocol choice based on current evidence — the cost doubles, but for injury management the potential upside is meaningful.

### Can collagen replace whey protein for muscle building?

No. Collagen is not a complete protein — it **lacks tryptophan**, an essential amino acid required for mTOR-mediated muscle protein synthesis signaling, and contains very low levels of leucine and branched-chain amino acids versus whey or casein. Collagen peptides should be taken in addition to your regular protein intake, not as a replacement. If you substitute collagen for whey, you risk inadequate leucine and tryptophan for muscle hypertrophy over time.

### How long does it take to see results from collagen supplementation?

Most positive clinical collagen trials run **8–12 weeks** before measuring outcomes. Connective tissue turnover is significantly slower than muscle protein synthesis — tendons and ligaments remodel on a months-long timescale. Subjective improvements in joint comfort may be noticeable within 4–6 weeks for some; objective structural changes take considerably longer and are not detectable without imaging. Be skeptical of influencers or products claiming noticeable results within a few days.

### Does the type of collagen (bovine vs. marine) matter for the pre-workout protocol?

For the tendon and ligament timing protocol, **bovine collagen (Types I and III)** is the better choice. Most pre-workout collagen research has used bovine-derived hydrolysate. Marine collagen provides primarily Type I collagen and is typically sold at doses of 5–7g per serving — well below the 15g clinical threshold, and more expensive per gram of collagen delivered. Marine collagen is better positioned for skin, hair, and nail applications where dosing thresholds are less stringent.
