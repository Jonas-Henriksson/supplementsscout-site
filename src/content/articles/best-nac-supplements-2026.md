NAC — N-acetyl-L-cysteine — has one of the longer clinical track records of any compound currently available as a supplement. Used pharmaceutically since the 1960s as a mucolytic and as the standard-of-care treatment for acetaminophen overdose, it occupies an unusual position: a drug-grade intervention that also functions as a wellness supplement. Consumer interest has grown significantly as the evidence base for respiratory health, liver protection, and behavioral applications has expanded.

Before buying, the regulatory context matters. As of April 2026, the FDA is exercising enforcement discretion on NAC as a dietary supplement — meaning sales continue while formal rulemaking works through the process. A proposed rule to authorize NAC as a supplement ingredient appeared in recent federal regulatory filings but had not been finalized as of late 2025. Products are widely available, but this is a space worth monitoring if you plan long-term use.

What follows is an evidence-grounded comparison of eight products across the full price spectrum — from $0.05/serving bulk powder to $0.55/serving sustained-release tablets — evaluated against clinical dose benchmarks, third-party testing credentials, and verified April 2026 pricing.

## What the Science Actually Says

NAC works as a cysteine donor. Cysteine is the rate-limiting substrate for glutathione synthesis — your primary endogenous antioxidant. When you supplement with NAC, you raise plasma cysteine availability, which increases intracellular glutathione production in the liver, lungs, and elsewhere. This mechanism is not in dispute.

**Respiratory evidence is the strongest category.** A Cochrane review found NAC at 600mg twice daily reduced exacerbation frequency in chronic bronchitis patients, with a number needed to treat of approximately six [Study: Poole et al., 2019, Cochrane Database]. The mucolytic mechanism is direct — NAC breaks disulfide bonds in mucus, reducing viscosity and improving airway clearance. This is the indication with the deepest human trial base.

**Liver support is well-supported and dose-dependent.** A placebo-controlled trial in non-alcoholic fatty liver disease used 600mg twice daily and found meaningful improvement in ALT and AST liver enzyme markers at 24 weeks [Study: Khoshbaten et al., 2024]. Glutathione depletion is a consistent feature of hepatic stress, and NAC addresses the upstream precursor deficit reliably at clinical doses.

**OCD and compulsive behaviors** are where things get clinically interesting. Multiple double-blind RCTs have found NAC reduces symptoms in OCD, trichotillomania, and body-focused repetitive behaviors. An RCT in *Biological Psychiatry* found **significant symptom reduction at 2,400mg/day over 12 weeks** [Study: Sarris et al., 2015]. A separate RCT for trichotillomania showed benefit at 1,200–2,400mg/day [Study: Grant et al., 2009]. Both exceed the standard 600mg capsule — directly relevant if this is your primary reason for supplementing.

**One safety caveat deserves clear placement**: animal studies showed accelerated tumor growth in mice with K-Ras/B-Raf mutations at doses around 1,200mg/day. The clinical significance in humans is actively debated and has not been replicated in human trials, but it is the reason most practitioners recommend staying at or below 600–1,200mg/day for general wellness use. This is a reason to stay within the evidence base — not a reason to avoid NAC at studied doses.

**The GlyNAC stack** has attracted serious attention for longevity applications. A 2023 pilot RCT in older adults found combined glycine and NAC supplementation improved mitochondrial function, insulin resistance, muscle strength, and cognitive markers over 24 weeks versus placebo [Study: Kumar et al., 2023, Nutrients]. The premise is that aging drives dual deficiencies in both cysteine and glycine, limiting glutathione synthesis. Results are preliminary — one small trial — but mechanistically grounded.

**What NAC probably doesn't do**: meaningfully improve athletic performance in well-nourished young adults. A 2020 review found no significant performance benefit in healthy trained athletes, likely because their baseline glutathione levels are already adequate.

## Dosing and Timing Guide

**Standard dose for most people: 600mg once daily.** This is the most consistently studied dose for respiratory support, liver health, and general antioxidant use. It's the right starting point before considering any higher protocol.

**Higher-dose protocols**: For OCD and body-focused repetitive behaviors, clinical trials used **1,200–2,400mg/day in two divided doses** [Study: Sarris et al., 2015; Grant et al., 2009]. If this is your primary indication, a single 600mg capsule is likely subtherapeutic based on the literature. Pursue higher doses with clinical oversight given the animal-model concerns at sustained elevated dosing.

**Timing**: NAC is water-soluble with no strong evidence-based timing window for general use. Taking it with food reduces GI upset risk — worth doing until you understand how your stomach responds. There is no meaningful argument for pre-workout or pre-sleep timing specifically.

If you use a pre-workout and want to avoid synthetic additives while stacking with NAC, see our guide to [Best Natural Pre-Workout 2026: No Artificial Sweeteners, Colors, or Flavors](/best-natural-pre-workout-2026).

**GlyNAC protocol**: The Kumar et al. (2023) trial used roughly 1.8g glycine and 0.9g NAC per 10kg body weight daily. A practical starting point for self-experimenting adults is 600mg NAC + 1,200mg glycine daily, though formal dose-finding data in healthy non-elderly populations is limited. BulkSupplements makes mixing practical; Pure Encapsulations offers a pre-blended powder.

**No loading protocol**: NAC does not require a loading phase like creatine. Consistent daily dosing from the start is appropriate.

**Duration**: Respiratory and liver trials ran 12–24 weeks before showing measurable benefit. Don't evaluate NAC on a 2-week trial. Behavioral applications showed effects at 8–12 weeks of consistent daily dosing.

## Who Should and Shouldn't Take NAC

**Most likely to benefit:**
- People with chronic bronchitis or COPD — this is the indication with the deepest human trial evidence
- Individuals with elevated liver enzymes, regular alcohol consumption, or high acetaminophen use
- Those managing OCD, trichotillomania, or body-focused repetitive behaviors — at 1,200–2,400mg with clinical oversight
- Older adults interested in glutathione and mitochondrial support via the GlyNAC stack
- People with chronic sinusitis or seasonal respiratory congestion

One user on WebMD, describing over a year of daily use for sinusitis: *"It has helped tremendously with my breathing and sinusitis...invaluable during seasonal allergy times to help reduce phlegm and improve respiratory function."* — WebMD Vitamins & Supplements Reviews

**Use with caution or consult your physician:**
- **Cancer history or active malignancy** — particularly anyone with known K-Ras or B-Raf mutations; the animal tumor-promotion data warrants explicit conversation with your oncologist before starting
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding** — NAC is used medically in pregnancy only for acute overdose emergencies; supplemental doses lack adequate safety data for this population
- **Asthma** — inhaled NAC is bronchodilatory, but oral supplemental doses occasionally trigger bronchospasm in susceptible individuals; start at 600mg cautiously
- **Nitroglycerin users** — NAC potentiates vasodilatory effects and can cause significant hypotension
- **Chemotherapy patients** — NAC may reduce the efficacy of certain chemotherapy agents; flagged in multiple oncology protocols

**Drug interactions worth flagging**: Potential additive anticoagulant effects with blood thinners at high doses. If you are on prescription medications — particularly cardiac, oncologic, or psychiatric drugs — discuss NAC with your prescriber before starting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does NAC actually do in the body?

NAC (N-acetyl-L-cysteine) provides cysteine, the rate-limiting substrate for glutathione synthesis. Glutathione is your primary endogenous antioxidant — it neutralizes reactive oxygen species, supports liver Phase II detoxification enzymes, and plays a role in immune regulation. At 600mg daily, NAC reliably elevates plasma cysteine levels; the degree to which this increases tissue glutathione depends on your baseline status, which tends to be lower in people with chronic illness, heavy alcohol use, or high medication burden.

### Is NAC legal to buy in the US in 2026?

Yes, as of April 2026. The FDA is exercising enforcement discretion — NAC continues to be sold as a supplement while formal rulemaking to authorize it as a dietary ingredient works through the regulatory process. A proposed rule appeared in recent federal filings but had not been finalized as of late 2025. Products are available through major retailers, Amazon, and directly from brands. This status can change; monitor FDA rulemaking updates if long-term use is central to your health plan.

### What is the right dose of NAC?

**600mg once daily** is the most consistently studied dose for respiratory, liver, and general antioxidant support. For OCD and compulsive behaviors, clinical trials used 1,200–2,400mg/day in divided doses [Study: Sarris et al., 2015; Grant et al., 2009]. Higher doses are best approached with clinical guidance given the animal-model tumor-promotion concerns at sustained elevated dosing.

### Should I take NAC with food?

Taking NAC with food reduces the likelihood of stomach upset, which some users report — particularly above 600mg. There is no strong evidence that food meaningfully affects NAC absorption. The practical recommendation is to take it with a meal until you understand how your stomach responds, then adjust based on experience.

### What is the GlyNAC stack?

GlyNAC combines NAC with glycine to address both cysteine and glycine deficiencies that limit glutathione synthesis in aging. A 2023 pilot RCT in older adults found GlyNAC improved mitochondrial function, insulin resistance, muscle strength, and cognitive markers over 24 weeks [Study: Kumar et al., 2023, Nutrients]. Results are preliminary — one small trial in an older population — but the mechanism is well-grounded. ### Why does NAC smell like sulfur?

NAC contains a thiol (-SH) group — the same functional group responsible for the smell of garlic, onions, and cooked eggs. This is a fundamental property of the cysteine molecule, not a formulation defect or a sign of spoilage. Every NAC product will have some degree of sulfur odor; powder is more pronounced than capsules. The smell has no bearing on potency or quality.

### Can I take NAC with other supplements or medications?

NAC pairs naturally with **glycine** (for the GlyNAC stack) and **vitamin C** for synergistic antioxidant support. Interactions worth knowing: NAC potentiates nitroglycerin — avoid combining if you use nitroglycerate for cardiac conditions. At doses of 1,200mg and above, there is potential for additive anticoagulant effects with blood thinners. NAC may reduce efficacy of certain chemotherapy agents — flagged in multiple oncology protocols. If you are on prescription medications, particularly cardiac, oncologic, or psychiatric drugs, discuss NAC with your prescriber before starting.
