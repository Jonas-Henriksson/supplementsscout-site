NAC — N-acetyl-L-cysteine — has one of the longer clinical track records of any compound you can currently buy as a supplement. It's been used pharmaceutically since the 1960s as a mucolytic and as the standard-of-care treatment for acetaminophen overdose. Consumer interest has grown significantly as the evidence base for respiratory health, liver support, and behavioral applications has deepened.

A proposed rule to authorize NAC as a supplement ingredient appeared in recent regulatory filings but had not been finalized as of late 2025. Products are widely available, but monitoring FDA updates is reasonable if you're planning long-term use.

## What the Science Actually Says

NAC works as a cysteine donor. Cysteine is the rate-limiting substrate for glutathione synthesis — your primary endogenous antioxidant. When cysteine supply is adequate, glutathione production increases, reducing oxidative stress in the liver, lungs, and elsewhere. NAC is a more bioavailable, stable form of cysteine than cysteine itself.

**Respiratory evidence is the strongest area**. A Cochrane review of 11 randomized controlled trials found NAC at **600mg twice daily** reduced exacerbation frequency in chronic bronchitis, with a number needed to treat of approximately 6 [Study: Poole et al., 2019]. The mucolytic mechanism is straightforward — NAC breaks disulfide bonds in mucus, reducing viscosity and improving clearance.

**Liver support is dose-dependent and well-supported**. A 2024 placebo-controlled trial in non-alcoholic fatty liver disease used 600mg twice daily (1,200mg total) and found meaningful improvement in ALT and AST liver enzymes at 24 weeks [Study: Khoshbaten et al., 2024]. Glutathione depletion is a consistent feature of hepatic stress, and NAC addresses it reliably at these doses.

**Behavioral and psychiatric applications are promising but preliminary**. A randomized controlled trial in *Biological Psychiatry* found 2,400mg/day over 12 weeks significantly reduced obsessive-compulsive symptom scores versus placebo [Study: Sarris et al., 2015]. For trichotillomania (hair-pulling disorder), an RCT using 1,200–2,400mg/day showed meaningful reduction in urges [Study: Grant et al., 2009]. Both of these doses exceed the standard 600mg daily capsule — relevant when choosing a protocol.

**The dose ceiling concern**: Research flagged by multiple clinical reviewers involves a rodent study showing accelerated tumor growth at approximately 1,200mg/day in mice with K-Ras or B-Raf mutations. The clinical significance in humans is genuinely debated — rodent pharmacokinetics differ substantially — but it's the reason most practitioners recommend staying at or below 1,200mg/day for general wellness use. It's also why the 600mg standard dose makes sense as a starting point.

The **GlyNAC stack** deserves mention for anyone interested in longevity applications. A 2023 pilot RCT in older adults found combined glycine and NAC supplementation improved mitochondrial function, insulin resistance, muscle strength, and cognitive markers over 24 weeks compared to placebo [Study: Kumar et al., 2023]. The premise: NAC addresses cysteine deficiency while glycine addresses the glycine deficiency common in aging — together they drive glutathione synthesis more reliably than either alone. Pure Encapsulations offers a pre-blended version; BulkSupplements makes the DIY approach practical.

**What NAC probably doesn't do**: Meaningfully improve athletic performance in well-nourished young adults. A 2020 review in *Nutrients* found no significant performance benefit in healthy trained athletes, likely because their baseline glutathione levels are adequate.

## Dosing and Timing Guide

**Standard starting dose**: **600mg once daily**. This is the dose used in the majority of respiratory, liver, and antioxidant trials and is appropriate for general wellness use. Most people should start here regardless of their end goal.

**Higher-dose protocols**: For OCD and body-focused repetitive behaviors, clinical trials have used **1,200–2,400mg/day in divided doses** [Study: Sarris et al., 2015; Grant et al., 2009]. These higher doses are best pursued under clinical guidance given the tumor-promotion concerns at sustained high doses in animal models. Do not jump to 2,400mg as a starting dose.

**Timing**: NAC has no strong evidence-based timing window. Taking it with food reduces the likelihood of stomach upset, particularly above 600mg. There is no meaningful argument for pre-workout or pre-sleep timing specifically. If you're stacking NAC with a pre-workout supplement, look for clean-label options — our [Best Natural Pre-Workout 2026](/best-natural-pre-workout-2026) guide covers formulations with no artificial additives.

**GlyNAC protocol**: The Kumar et al. 2023 trial used roughly 1.8g glycine and 0.9g NAC per 10kg body weight daily. A practical starting point for self-experimenting adults is 600mg NAC + 1,200mg glycine daily, though formal dose-finding studies in healthy populations are limited. BulkSupplements makes custom mixing practical; Pure Encapsulations offers a pre-blended powder.

**Duration**: Respiratory and liver trials ran 12–24 weeks before showing measurable benefit. Don't assess NAC by a two-week trial. The behavioral applications also show effects at 8–12 weeks of consistent daily dosing.

**Loading protocols**: Not applicable. There is no evidence supporting a loading phase for NAC, unlike creatine. Consistent daily dosing from the start.

## Who Should and Shouldn't Take NAC

**Most likely to benefit:**
- People with chronic respiratory conditions — chronic bronchitis, COPD, frequent sinusitis. This is the strongest evidence category.
- Individuals with elevated liver enzymes, regular alcohol consumption, or high medication burden that stresses hepatic glutathione
- Those managing OCD, trichotillomania, or body-focused repetitive behaviors (dermatillomania) — at 1,200–2,400mg, ideally with clinical oversight
- Older adults interested in glutathione and mitochondrial support via the GlyNAC stack
- People with regular acetaminophen use — NAC supports the hepatic glutathione that acetaminophen depletes at therapeutic doses

**Use with caution or avoid:**
- **Cancer history or active malignancy** — particularly anyone with known K-Ras or B-Raf mutations; the animal tumor-promotion data warrants an explicit conversation with your oncologist before starting
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding** — NAC is used medically in pregnancy only for acetaminophen overdose emergencies; supplemental doses lack adequate safety data in this population
- **Asthma** — inhaled NAC is bronchodilatory, but oral supplemental doses occasionally provoke bronchospasm in susceptible individuals; start cautiously
- **Nitroglycerin users** — NAC potentiates nitroglycerin's vasodilatory effects and can cause significant hypotension and severe headache
- **Pre-surgery** — discuss with your surgeon; NAC's effects on oxidative metabolism may interact with anesthetic protocols

**Drug interactions worth knowing**: Potential additive anticoagulant effects with blood thinners at high doses. NAC may reduce the efficacy of certain chemotherapy agents — this is flagged in multiple oncology protocols. If you are on any prescription medications, particularly cardiac, oncologic, or psychiatric medications, review NAC with your prescriber before starting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does NAC actually do in the body?

NAC (N-acetyl-L-cysteine) provides cysteine, the rate-limiting substrate for glutathione synthesis. Glutathione is your primary endogenous antioxidant — it neutralizes reactive oxygen species, supports liver Phase II detoxification enzymes, and plays a role in immune regulation. At 600mg daily, NAC reliably elevates plasma cysteine levels; the degree to which this increases tissue glutathione depends on your baseline status, which tends to be lower in people with chronic illness, heavy alcohol use, or high medication burden.

### Is NAC legal to buy in the US in 2026?

Yes, as of April 2026. The FDA is exercising enforcement discretion — NAC continues to be sold as a supplement while formal rulemaking to authorize it as a dietary ingredient works through the regulatory process. A proposed rule appeared in recent regulatory filings but had not been finalized as of late 2025. Products are available through major retailers, Amazon, and direct from brands. Monitor FDA rulemaking updates if long-term use is central to your health plan.

### What is the right dose of NAC?

**600mg once daily** is the most studied dose for respiratory, liver, and general antioxidant support. For OCD and compulsive behaviors, clinical trials used 1,200–2,400mg/day in divided doses [Study: Sarris et al., 2015; Grant et al., 2009] — higher doses are best approached with clinical guidance given the animal-model concerns at sustained elevated dosing.

### Should I take NAC with food?

Taking NAC with food reduces the likelihood of stomach upset, which some users report on an empty stomach — particularly above 600mg. There is no strong evidence that food meaningfully affects NAC absorption. The practical recommendation is to take it with a meal until you understand how your stomach responds.

### What is the GlyNAC stack and does it work?

GlyNAC combines NAC with glycine, addressing both deficiencies that limit glutathione synthesis in aging. A 2023 pilot RCT in older adults found GlyNAC improved mitochondrial function, insulin resistance, muscle strength, and cognitive markers over 24 weeks [Study: Kumar et al., 2023]. Results are preliminary — the study population was small and older adults — but the mechanism is well-grounded. BulkSupplements makes DIY mixing practical; Pure Encapsulations offers a pre-blended powder option.

### Why does NAC smell like sulfur?

NAC contains a thiol (-SH) group — the same functional group responsible for the smell of garlic, onions, and cooked eggs. This is a fundamental property of the cysteine molecule, not a formulation defect or sign of spoilage. Every NAC product will have some degree of sulfur odor. Sealed capsules contain it reasonably well; powder is more noticeable. The smell has no bearing on potency or quality.

### Can I take NAC with other supplements?

NAC pairs naturally with **glycine** (GlyNAC stack), **vitamin C** (synergistic antioxidant support), and standard multivitamins. The interactions worth knowing: NAC potentiates nitroglycerin — avoid combining if you use nitroglycerin for cardiac conditions. At high doses (1,200mg and above), there is potential for additive anticoagulant effects with blood thinners. NAC may reduce efficacy of certain chemotherapy agents. If you are on prescription medications, particularly cardiac or oncologic drugs, discuss NAC with your prescriber before starting.
